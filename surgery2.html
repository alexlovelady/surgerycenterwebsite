Overview

Knee arthroscopy is a surgical procedure that allows doctors to view the knee joint without making a large incision through the skin and other soft tissues. It is commonly used to diagnose and treat a variety of knee problems, including torn meniscus, damaged cartilage, and inflamed joint linings.

Statistics

  • More than 4 million knee arthroscopies are performed worldwide each year.
  • The procedure has a high success rate, with over 90% of patients reporting significant improvement in knee function.
  • Recovery time varies, but most patients can return to normal activities within 4 to 6 weeks.

What to Expect

Pre-Operative Instructions

Patients should stop taking certain medications, such as blood thinners, a few days before surgery. Fasting is required 8 hours before the procedure. Arrangements should be made for someone to take you home after surgery.

The Procedure

The surgery is typically performed under regional or general anesthesia. Small incisions are made around the knee, and a camera (arthroscope) is inserted to guide the surgery. Depending on the findings, the surgeon may repair or remove damaged tissue.

Post-Operative Care

Post-operative care involves rest, ice, compression, and elevation (RICE) to manage swelling and pain. Physical therapy may be recommended to help restore knee function. Follow-up visits will be scheduled to monitor your recovery.
